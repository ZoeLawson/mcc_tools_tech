Allergies

Detecting Allergies / Sensitivities / Intolerances:

In addition to allergy testing by an allergist, you may notice sensitivities and intolerances on your own with careful record-keeping. The food section in the Daily Record may help with this.

The most common way to determine sensitivities is to avoid the suspect food(s) for a certain amount of time (such as 30 days) and then re-introducing the food as a challenge and see what happens. This is best undertaken under medical supervision.

Randy Horwitz, M.D., Ph.D., medical director of the Arizona Center for Integrative Medicine at the University of Arizona, who specializes in immunology “prefers to ask patients to keep a record for a few weeks of everything they eat and any symptoms that develop in response to specific foods.  This can help narrow the list of foods that may be causing problems.  The next step is a defined food elimination diet.  This can be an avoidance diet of patient-defined triggers, a "hypoallergenic" diet for four to six weeks, or a rotation diet, in which new foods are introduced sequentially.  Once symptoms have been associated with a food or food group, the intolerance can be confirmed with a ‘challenge’ in which the patient is given the suspect food and then watched to see if symptoms develop (This isn't practical when symptoms are severe).” Source: http://www.drweil.com/drw/u/QAA400354/Best-Test-for-Food-Intolerance.html

If you are still experiencing symptoms that don’t seem to resolve after testing most foods and food families, you may want to start looking at the following factors:

  • Was the food cooked and how?

  • Was it homemade or processed, pre-packaged?

  • Can you get a recipe or complete ingredient list?  If possible, speak to the person who cooked the food.

  • Did you eat the food someplace other than home?  Where?

  • What is the brand?

  • What was the amount you ate?

  • Were there any seeds, sauces or spices? 

  • Was there a spray or oil used on the cooking surface?

  • Did you have any contact with animals, latex, pollen, perfumes, mold, cigarette smoke, fresh paint, or cleaning chemicals?

  • Did you take any medication for symptoms such as Benadryl or Alka-Seltzer Gold?  Was it helpful?

Foods in a Hurry

You don’t feel good but you need to eat!  AND, it needs to support your health.  Follow your dietary needs and restrictions when choosing which foods might work for you.  Often being prepared ahead of time is key.  When you are feeling up to it, spend a little extra time preparing healthy snacks.  The freezer can be your friend!

Here are a few foods I use that fit with my dietary needs.  This is just to give you a few ideas:

  •  

  • Beef jerky

  • Nuts or Seeds

  • Fruits (raw, dried, frozen)

  • Raw veggies and dip

  • Square of dark chocolate

  • Cup of bone broth (prepped ahead of time!)

  • Boiled Egg

  • Smoothie

  • Home-made healthy muffin

  • Slice of deli meat

  • Plantain, zucchini, kale chips

  • Lettuce wrap

  • Avocado/Guacamole

  • Salad
